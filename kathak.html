Kathak

Introduction to Kathak

Kathak is a classical dance form that originated in North India and is traditionally attributed to the traveling bards of ancient northern India known as Kathakars or storytellers. The name 'Kathak' is derived from the Sanskrit word 'katha' meaning story, and thus Kathak is 'the one who tells a story'. It is distinguished by its intricate footwork and precise rhythmic patterns that the dancers execute on the beat of the music, and its unique use of facial expressions and hand gestures that convey the emotions and storyline of the performance.

Historical Roots

Tracing its origins to the nomadic bards of ancient India, Kathak evolved during the Bhakti movement, particularly by incorporating the childhood and life stories of Hindu god Krishna, as well as independently in the courts of North Indian kingdoms. These regions include what are today the states of Rajasthan, Uttar Pradesh, and Bihar, where Kathak developed in three distinct forms known as 'gharanas': the Jaipur, Banaras, and Lucknow gharanas, each noted for its particular style and emphasisâ€”whether it be on footwork, spins, or abhinaya (the expression of thematic content).

Performance and Techniques

A Kathak performance typically begins with slow, graceful movements that gradually build up speed and complexity, often culminating in a fast-paced finale that showcases the performer's skill and training. The dance is performed by both men and women, and can be solo or in groups. Key elements include intricate footwork, spins (chakkars), and the dramatic representation of themes from Persian and Urdu poetry alongside those of Hindu mythology. The costume worn by the dancers is usually a sari for women, adapted in a way that reflects the Mughal influence on the art form.

The music typically associated with Kathak is based on the Hindustani style of classical music. Instruments commonly used are the tabla (for rhythmic support), the sarangi or harmonium (for melodic accompaniments), and occasionally, the sitar or sarod. The vocal music primarily comprises compositions in Hindi or Brij Bhasha and involves complex cycles of rhythm (taal) that the dancer interprets through their performances, often mimicking the percussion patterns with rapid foot movements.

Cultural Significance

Today, Kathak remains a popular and influential art form in India and around the world. It is practiced extensively and continues to evolve, incorporating elements of contemporary and global dance forms. Kathak dancers are highly regarded for their agility, control, and grace, and the dance itself is celebrated for its beauty, storytelling capabilities, and the intricate interplay of rhythmic foot movements, hand gestures, and facial expressions.
